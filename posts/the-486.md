I was seven years old and there was a beige box in the house.

Mid-nineties. Intel 486 processor. My dad was switched on, he knew what it was, knew how to use it. I just knew it had a Lion King demo on it and I was going to play it until someone made me stop.

That was the beginning. I just didn't know it yet.

![An Intel 486 PC, the beige box that started everything](/images/486-pc.jpg)

## Full Throttle

![Full Throttle, 1995, LucasArts](/images/full-throttle.jpg)

Eventually I found a game called Full Throttle. A biker. A Harley. A story I was probably too young for and completely obsessed with. I got stuck in that junkyard so many times. It led me to Fallout 1, which led me somewhere I didn't expect.

I figured out how to copy it.

One disc drive to a writable drive. Burn the image. Sleeve it. Sell it to kids at school for money. Money to buy new Tech Decks.

Was it illegal? Yeah. Was it impressive for a twelve-year-old? Absolutely.

That's not the point. The point is what copying Fallout 1 required me to understand. You couldn't just click through it. The machine didn't want to do it. You had to go somewhere the interface didn't show you, learn something the manual didn't explain, and make the computer do something it wasn't obviously set up to do.

That was the first time I understood that computers aren't click-and-open things. There's something behind what you see.

![Fallout 1, the game that made me go deeper](/images/fallout-1.jpg)

## The Question That Didn't Stop

Once you know there's a layer underneath, you can't unknow it. Is that even a word?

*How does this work, though?*

Asked that at twelve in front of a beige box. Still asking it. The tools changed. The curiosity didn't.

There's always something behind what you see. A model behind the interface. Infrastructure behind the model. Assumptions behind all of it.

You can use the surface, or you can pull at it...

## The Through Line

I didn't choose to work in technology. I just never stopped being the kid who needed to know how it worked.

The 486 gave me a machine I didn't understand. Full Throttle gave me a reason to. Fallout 1 made me go further. I never came back.

Thirty years later. Different box. Same question.

*There's something behind what you see.*

There always is. Now that curiosity is my career. The job is pointing that same instinct at real problems. Systems that don't make sense. Processes that should be faster. Questions nobody's asked yet because they're too busy using the surface.

That skill pays my bills. It bought me a house. Cars. Things I could only dream of as a kid. It supports my family. It pays for my life.

That 486 started everything.
