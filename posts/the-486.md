I was seven years old and there was a beige box in the house.

Mid-nineties. Intel 486 processor. My dad was switched on, he knew what it was, knew how to use it. I just knew it had a Lion King demo on it and I was going to play it until someone made me stop.

That was the beginning. I just didn't know it yet.

![An Intel 486 PC — the beige box that started everything](/images/486-pc.jpg)

## Full Throttle

![Full Throttle — 1995, LucasArts](/images/full-throttle.jpg)

Eventually I found a game called Full Throttle. A biker. A Harley. A story I was probably too young for and completely obsessed with, man I got stuck in that junk yard so many times. I think that game taught me so many lessons about gaming. It led me to Fallout 1, which led me somewhere I didn't expect.

I figured out how to copy it.

One disc drive to a writable drive. Burn the image. Sleeve it. Sell it to kids at school for money. Money to buy new Tech Decks.

Was it illegal? Yes. Was it impressive for a twelve-year-old? Also yes.

But that's not actually the point. The point is what copying Fallout 1 required me to understand. You couldn't just click through it. The machine didn't want to do it. You had to go somewhere the interface didn't show you, learn something the manual didn't explain, and make the computer do something it wasn't obviously set up to do.

That was the first time I understood that computers aren't click-and-open things. There's something behind what you see.

![Fallout 1 — the game that made me go deeper](/images/fallout-1.jpg)

## The Question That Didn't Stop

Once you know there's a layer underneath, you can't unknow it. Is that even a word?

*How does this work, though?*

That question, I asked at twelve in front of a beige box trying to figure out disc imaging, is the same question I'm asking now in front of AI infrastructure that would have looked like science fiction to that kid. The tools changed but the curiosity didn't. The itch is identical.

There's always something behind what you see. A model behind the interface. A system behind the model. A decision behind the system. An assumption behind the decision. Infrastructure behind the whole stack.

You can use the surface, or you can pull at it...

## The Through Line

I didn't choose to work in technology. I just never stopped being the kid who needed to know how it worked. It's like technology chose me.

The 486 gave me a machine I didn't fully understand. Full Throttle gave me a reason to understand it. Fallout 1 gave me a reason to go deeper than understanding it. And somewhere in that sequence, a switch flipped that never switched back.

Thirty years later the box is different but the question is the same.

*There's something behind what you see.*

There always is. Now that curiosity is my career. The problem isn't working out how computers work, it's taking the same problem-solving instinct I've been sharpening my whole life and pointing it at real-world problems. Systems that don't make sense. Processes that should be faster. Questions nobody's asked yet because they're too busy using the surface.

That skill pays my bills. It bought me a house. Cars. Things I could only dream of as a kid. It supports my family. It pays for my life.

I am forever grateful for that 486.
