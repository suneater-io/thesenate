# Grant Hansen — Everything I Know

*Written from conversation. Honest, unfiltered, complete.*

---

## The Short Version

Solution Architect. Father. Australian with Norwegian blood. Bleeds Detroit. Over 50 hours in the tattoo chair. Death metal obsessive since age 8. IGL since 13. Horror obsessive since childhood. Built everything from scratch. Earned all of it.

---

## Who He Actually Is

Grant is someone who doesn't move until he knows. Then he goes all the way in. This pattern shows up everywhere — the 12 years between his first tattoo and starting his sleeves, the career pivot after the band split, the marriage he fought to keep instead of walking away from. He thinks before he acts. When he acts, it's total commitment.

He is calm under pressure. When something goes wrong his internal response is: be calm, think about it, work it out, respond appropriately. This isn't a coping strategy he learned. It's just who he is. It's the IGL in real life.

He describes himself as a chameleon but doesn't want that label. The truer version: the same person in the middle of a pit on a Wednesday night is the same person in the room with the CEO. He doesn't change who he is. He just knows how to find the human part of anyone. This is rare, especially in someone with his kind of intensity.

His personality is, by his own description, wild. His kids tame it. That might be the most revealing thing he's said about himself.

## The Origin

Age 7. Parents' 486. Lion King demo. That was the beginning.

He wasn't the kind of kid who just played games. He was the kind who needed to understand the machine. Full Throttle led to Fallout 1 which led to figuring out how to copy it — one disc drive to a writable drive, sleeve it, sell it to kids at school for money to buy Tech Decks. Illegal. Also impressive for a twelve-year-old. The point wasn't the copying. The point was what it required him to understand. That computers weren't click-and-open things. That there's always something behind what you see.

That instinct never left. It just scaled. From a 486 to enterprise AI infrastructure. Same question, bigger machine.

## Music

Music is not something he does. It's what he is. His words.

It started at 8 and never stopped. Metallica and Slayer first. By 12 it was Dying Fetus, Morbid Angel, Cannibal Corpse. At 14, Meshuggah. That was the Matrix moment.

Not the music — the philosophy. Something existed that broke every rule of music and was better for it. Controlled chaos. Syncopated, relentless, and somehow in perfect control. That's exactly how his brain works, by his own description. When he heard Meshuggah it didn't just change his taste. It changed his reality. It showed him that rules are starting points, not ceilings.

He plays drums. He's been on stage. He goes to 6-10 shows a year minimum. He is a deep diver — goes down rabbit holes and doesn't come back. He believes music is more important to people's lives than most people let themselves admit. Not background noise. A force.

**The roster that defines him:** Meshuggah (#1, always), The Black Dahlia Murder, Dying Fetus, Cattle Decapitation, Between the Buried and Me, Devin Townsend, Pantera, Slayer, Behemoth.

## The Band

At 15 he joined a band that did well. Played big stages with Parkway Drive and I Killed the Prom Queen. Recorded EPs. Signed a label deal. The album never came — the singer left to tour with I Killed the Prom Queen and they never recovered. They agreed to part ways. He put his focus into his career.

He was 15. The experience taught him partying, the live music scene, touring mixed with school and work and navigating teen life. He doesn't talk about it as a loss. He talks about it as an education. The band ended and something new began. That's the Ragnarök pattern before he had the language for it.

## The Tattoos

First one: Meshuggah logo on his arm at 18. Then nothing for 12 years.

He told himself full sleeves were a commitment he needed to be certain about. At 30 he was certain. He started.

Both arm sleeves. Left leg sleeve. Stomach. Chest. Over 50 hours total. The themes: Norse mythology, music, Star Wars, horror. Nothing random. Everything chosen.

The left arm is the one he's most proud of. The full story of Ragnarök in Norse style illustration, written in runes, with Jörmungandr — the world serpent — wrapping the entire arm. It's not a tattoo. It's a manuscript. A belief system made visible.

The fight that hits him hardest is Fenrir. The sun eater. The giant wolf, son of Loki, eating the sun. He can't explain why exactly. It's a feeling the story gives him. Some things can't be explained — they're felt. That's the whole point.

## The Ragnarök Philosophy

Ragnarök is the end of everything. The gods know it's coming. They fight anyway.

For him it's not about the complete story. It's about each respective fight. What resonates is this: nothing is free, nothing is given, everything is earned. He will face fights he may lose. Even in loss something new can be created or born. The important part is the fight — taking the fight and not fearing it.

This is not an abstract philosophy. It's how he has actually lived. The band ended and a career started. The dark place happened and the marriage came out stronger. Every ending has been a beginning. He doesn't fear the end of things. He engages with them.

## Counter-Strike

Playing since 13. Never stopped.

Not because of the shooting. Because of everything else. The reads. The strats. The way you can study five rounds and know exactly what's coming on round six. He plays IGL — calls the plays, writes the setups, reads the map, reads the enemy, makes the call in real time. When his team doesn't know what to do they look at him. That's where he belongs.

The IGL role fits his personality perfectly. It's the same instinct that makes him calm in a crisis, that makes him the person people look to in a room. The technical depth of the game is bottomless. He has never found the bottom.

CS:S era: him and his brother landed in a clan playing Game Arena ranked ladder in Australia. Hit 7th in the country. He had printouts of maps on his desk, drawing strategies by hand. Didn't have a name for what he was doing. Learned early that raw mechanical skill without buy-in is useless — and that you earn buy-in by putting people in positions where they succeed, not by trying to change them.

**The demo review practice** is what he credits most. Before every session, after every session — win or loss — you review the footage from above. Birds-eye view. Detached. What could have been done better? This became a life habit. He applies it to work, relationships, decisions. Most people only reflect on failure. He reflects on everything. He frames it as the most transferable skill the game gave him.

**The professional transfer is direct.** Major Sev 1 incident — full hospital network down. He leads the call the same way he leads a round. Stay composed, read the situation, put the right people on the right problems. "Engineer 1 to Core Switch 1, same way I'd call my brother to entry Dust2 long pop." The IGL set the temperature of the room in the game. He does it in the room.

This clicked explicitly when he interviewed for his role at ArchiTech. Behavioral interview questions — give me an example of leading through a challenging situation, handling pressure — and every answer he had was Counter-Strike. He told them straight: 5000 hours, IGL, learned to lead different personality types with different communication styles toward the same goal, with people he'd never met, against problems he knew nothing about, on the fly. They might not know what CS is. They know what that is.

**CS as sport, not just game.** ESL Challenger events 2023/24/25. IEM Sydney 2023. IEM Melbourne 2025. He's been to all of it. At IEM Sydney he met TC — coach of Complexity at the time — and it turned into a real friendship. They stayed in contact, met again at IEM Sydney for the Complexity vs FaZe grand final (FaZe won). TC now leads PassionUA. They still talk.

Being in that arena is something else. 5000 hours behind your eyes means you're not just watching — you see the IGL's call before it happens, you understand what every decision cost to execute. FaZe winning IEM Sydney was one of the greatest things he's ever witnessed. His word: life-changing.

## Detroit Sports

He is not from Detroit. He chose it.

Collecting since 2007. Pistons, Red Wings, Tigers, Lions. Cards, jerseys, memorabilia — not as an investment, as devotion. He chose those teams. He stayed loyal when they gave him every reason to leave. The Pistons lost a record 27 games straight, he watched them all. Not because he thought they'd turn it around. Because they were the Pisstons and watching was what you did.

Detroit represents something to him. Working hard and enjoying the rewards when it pays off. You can only truly feel success if you've experienced failure. The city lives this. He identified with it enough to make it his own even from a distance.

The Red Wings dynasty, Pistons, the decades of losing — it all built something. The ability to stay committed to something that isn't paying off yet. Loyalty that only holds when the team is winning isn't loyalty. It's tourism.

## Technology

Impossible to separate where the passion ends and the job begins. That's probably the point.

Parents' 486 at 7. The curiosity that never went away. First PC support role that confirmed what he already suspected. Has always built his own gaming PCs, always chased the latest hardware. AI has made the passion louder. The technology landscape grows and so does the pull toward it.

Solution Architect. The career was built from scratch. Everything he knows came from pulling at things until he understood them.

People come to him for his solution set — AI, Webex Contact Centre, integrated health — but also for direction, inspiration and knowledge. He's not just the technical person in the room. He's the one people look to for where to go next.

At his best: collaborating, planning, strategising, designing. And bringing smiles and laughs. He said both in the same breath without hesitation. That combination is rarer than most people admit.

He knew he was good at this when he completed projects at scale across multiple technologies at a previous role and did it well. Not a single moment of genius — proof through execution.

What he wants people to say when he's not in the room: "He was fun to work with and we always did great work." No ego in it. Just the work and the people.

**What bad leadership looks like to him:** Not getting to know your people. Not knowing what your people do day to day. Not giving people room and space to think and manage their workload. Not actually listening. Every one of these is about people, not results. He leads from human connection first.

## Norwegian Heritage & Norse Philosophy

His great-grandparents emigrated from Norway to Australia. He grew up proud of that heritage. As he got older the pride deepened. That's how he found Norse mythology — not through pop culture or TV shows, through his own bloodline. That distinction matters enormously. The Ragnarök sleeve written in runes on his left arm is not a design choice. It's ancestry made permanent.

He doesn't believe in religion. He doesn't believe in gods. But he believes in nature, in being kind to the world, in treating it with respect. That's what Norse paganism was actually founded on — a relationship with the natural world, not a set of commandments. He found his ancestral tradition and it turned out to align exactly with what he already believed.

What it gives him: peace. Not excitement, not identity — peace. This is the still layer underneath everything else. All the intensity, the extreme music, the horror, the pressure decisions, the dark places — this is what sits under all of it. A quiet respect for the world and where he comes from.

The Hávamál — the sayings of Odin — is the text he returns to. "Cattle die, kinsmen die, the self must also die; but glory never dies for the man who achieves it." That verse is the easter egg hidden in the About page for anyone who finds it. It's not decoration. It's the operating manual.

## Horror

Grew up on Halloween, Nightmare on Elm Street and Friday the 13th. That's the baseline. It never left.

Somewhere along the way the fear response stopped working. No jump scares, no flinching. Most people would take that as a reason to stop watching. For him it turned into an obsession with the craft, the atmosphere, the darkness underneath. He watches everything now — psychological, slashers, gore, torture horror. The more uncomfortable the better. He loves that it's taboo, dark, sad, unsettling. The thing most people won't sit with.

**Michael Myers** is the definitive film. Not because of the kills — because of the calm. Myers doesn't run, doesn't rage. He just moves. The stillness amongst the chaos. You never knew if he was human. That question is the whole film. This is the same quality Grant identifies with in himself — the IGL who doesn't panic, the calm in the room. He identified it in a horror villain before he had language for it in himself.

**Terrifier 3** is his pick for best recent horror. Feels like classic nostalgic slasher but modern. The whole film is uncomfortable in the best way. Art the Clown shouldn't be loveable. He is. That contradiction is what makes him work. Myers is composed stillness. Art is anarchic chaos. He's drawn to both ends of that spectrum for different reasons.

**The scene that has never left him:** The ending of The Devil's Rejects. The Firefly family driving toward a police roadblock. Free Bird playing. They know what's coming. They drive anyway. Something about it hits different every time. (Note: the Ragnarök parallel is clear — he didn't frame it that way himself, but the pull toward that specific scene is consistent with the same thread running through everything else.)

**Under-seen recommendation:** Bring Her Back. Psychological horror. He describes it as heavy. It stuck with him. The psychological ones land differently even though jump scares don't work on him — they hit something else.

**The leg sleeve:** Michael Myers, Freddy Krueger, Jason Voorhees. The holy trinity. They earned it.
